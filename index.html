Real World Constraints on Data Collection

My father is a physicist. He is obviously very bright, and this is possibly why he always insisted on giving a post-movie analysis regarding the believability of movie plots. I am envisioning his reaction to a movie entitled "Vaccine," in which a sinister government collects and stores information about their citizens by administering a vaccine during a pandemic. He would spend the hour after we had seen the movie describing in great detail just how ludicrous the plot was: (1) this technology does not exist, (2) even if it did--where is all this data going to be stored, and (3) having a ton of data on hundreds of millions of people isn't very useful unless you have the man-power to analyze it. I, on the other hand, am willing to suspend my disbelief to enjoy a good movie. But I am quite frankly baffled at the number of people who apparently accept as truth the notion that the COVID vaccine (or any other vaccine for that matter) is capable of transmitting biometric data to wifi towers. I did a little bit of Googling to try to figure out where this idea came from, and this is what I learned. One of the challenges of vaccinating people in poorer countries is that most do not have the ability to keep a good record of the vaccines that have been administered. Researchers at MIT are trying to address this critical issue by working on a special invisible dye that could be delivered along with a vaccine. This dye would contain nanocrystals that emit near-infrared light that would create something akin to a very tiny QR code tatoo that could be detected by a specially equipped smartphone. So let's review. There are researchers who are trying to create a way to use a vaccine's ingredients to store data, but the information they want to store is about the delivery of the vaccine itself, which is critical information when it is part of a multi-dose regimen. It is important to note that the research is still in the experimental stage at this point and has not has not been tested on humans. (On a side note, this research was partially funded by the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, which has a history of funding research on vaccines to prevent illnesse--such as malari--in poorer countries.) Two Truths and a Lie

"There are lies, damn lies, and statistics"

This phrase, which is often attributed to Mark Twain, was a favorite of my Uncle Richard. As someone with extensive training in statistics and econometrics--I have also learned that while data analysis is extremely useful--it is important to be aware of potential biases and weaknesses in the analysis. Let me give you one very simple example. NOAA has a dataset on the economic and human impacts of weather events. I have written three statements below. Can you spot the lie?

Statement 1: Tornados kill more people than any other weather event.

Statement 2: Tsunamies are a big danger in the US Midwest.

Statement 3: Extreme heat kill more people than any other weather event.


Which would did you get was incorrect. I bet you choose statement 2, but that is not correct. Statement 1 is the one that is incorrect. While the database started in 1950, until 1996 tornados, thunderstorms, wind and hail were the only events in the database. If you were to run a query on the data to find out the most deadly type of weather event, you would conclude that tornadoes are the most deadly. However, if you filter the data to include only those years that include the entire group of weather events, you would find that extreme heat causes the most deaths. Extreme head is the number one killer, which we was in evidence this past year. Although the midwest doesn't seem like it would have tsunamis, the Great Lakes have a common type of tsunami called a "meteotsunamis", which is kind of like bath water sloshing around it a bathtub.

About Me


Hi, I'm Kathy and this website is about how we as a society are collecting and processing increasingly greater amounts of data--or information--about every aspect of our lives.


See Profile

"Perfect information" (a state of the world in which people can easily and accurately obtain information) is essential for a well functioning market economy.

In some ways, the development of the web has given us an unprecedented ability to access information. The proliferation of websites dedicated to online learning for youth, such as Khan Academy, and the multitude of sites where you are able to learn from world-class professors--moocs such as Edx and Coursera, free online courses provided by University of Michigan's Dr. Chuck, and youtube channels, such as John Hopkin's Roger Peng teaching R, NYU's Daniel Siffman teaching Processing at his channel The Coding Train, as well as Chris Ching teaching Swift at CodeWithChris --are just a few examples that I have personally used.

Search engines such as Google allow us to find and compare products and services easily. I am no longer surprised when I imagine a new product idea, and can invariably I can find the product already for sale on the web.

All of this amazing technology comes with a cost. The price is the marketing data that is being collected about us. I am going to explore how this information is currently being collected and used--for good or ill--and how you can make informed decisions based on the available data.
